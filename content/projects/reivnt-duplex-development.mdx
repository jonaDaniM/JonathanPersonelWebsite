## Summary

I acquired and completed a partially built Houston duplex that had been abandoned by the previous owner/builder after multiple permitting, inspection, sitework, and documentation issues. The project required more than basic construction management. It required due diligence, public-record research, permit transfer coordination, contractor replacement, inspection recovery, cost control, and a clear investment thesis.

The property was originally listed at $210,000, later reduced to $199,000, then $179,000. After identifying the project's risks and understanding why other buyers were hesitant, I negotiated the purchase price down to $150,000. I completed the project all-in for approximately $270,000, including my builder fee, and later refinanced the property at approximately $399,000.

The outcome was a successful real estate investment: investor capital was repaid, I retained approximately $24,000-$25,000 in cash after refinance, preserved over $100,000 in equity, and positioned the property to cash flow immediately.

## The Opportunity

The property was listed on HAR as a partially completed duplex with visible signs of distress. It had framing in place, but the project had been mismanaged and left inactive for roughly a year to a year and a half. The site was overgrown, the grading had not been completed, and the construction workspace was not ready for efficient execution.

Most buyers saw the risk. I saw a distressed construction project that could become a strong investment if the issues were properly understood, priced into the acquisition, and managed through completion.

## Initial Problems Identified

The project had several major issues before acquisition:

- The previous owner/builder had failed or skipped key inspections.
- Some inspection steps had to be retraced because work had been covered too early.
- Siding had been installed before certain required inspections, forcing prior work to be reopened.
- The firewall had not yet passed, which created a serious compliance risk in the City of Houston.
- The property was near a flood zone and lacked the proper FEMA/flood-zone documentation.
- The previous owner had to pursue an exemption and pay additional fees to address flood-related requirements.
- Grading and sitework had not been completed.
- The lot was overgrown and poorly maintained after the project sat inactive.
- The permitting status was unclear.
- Existing contractors had to be removed or replaced on the city permits.
- The real estate agent and seller were not fully transparent about the project's technical issues.

The opportunity was only attractive if these problems could be converted from unknown risks into manageable execution items.

## Due Diligence Approach

Because the seller and agent were not volunteering the full picture, I conducted my own due diligence through the City of Houston. I submitted a public information request and used the records to reconstruct the project's history, inspection status, permitting issues, and unresolved requirements.

That research gave me roughly 90% of the information I needed to understand the risk. I was comfortable moving forward without the remaining 10% because the purchase price, construction budget, and pro forma created enough margin to justify the project.

This was one of the most important parts of the deal. I did not rely only on seller representations. I used public records, inspection history, city documentation, and financial modeling to make the acquisition decision.

## Acquisition Strategy

The property was originally listed at $210,000. After sitting on the market and facing buyer hesitation, it dropped to $199,000, then $179,000. I waited, studied the project, identified the technical problems, and negotiated the purchase price down to $150,000.

The discount was not accidental. It reflected the market's uncertainty around the permitting, inspection, construction, and sitework risks. My advantage was being able to understand those risks well enough to price them, manage them, and execute through them.

## My Role

I acted as the developer, operator, and project coordinator. My role included:

- Evaluating the acquisition opportunity.
- Building the pro forma.
- Negotiating the purchase price.
- Coordinating financing and investor capital.
- Investigating city permitting and inspection history.
- Transferring permits under my ownership/control.
- Removing previous contractors from permits where necessary.
- Adding my contractors to continue the work.
- Coordinating trades and suppliers.
- Managing grading, site cleanup, and construction readiness.
- Tracking open issues, inspections, contractor responsibilities, and cost decisions.
- Driving the project from distressed partial construction to completed income-producing asset.

## Operating System and Automation

To manage the project, I used a structured project-control system built around spreadsheet workflows and automation logic. The purpose was to treat the duplex like an operating project, not just a construction job.

The system tracked:

- Contractor contacts and responsibilities.
- Open tasks by trade.
- Inspection requirements and status.
- RFIs and unresolved issues.
- Grading, sitework, and city closeout items.
- Procurement and supplier follow-ups.
- Budget items and disputed charges.
- Follow-up reminders for contractors and suppliers.
- Documentation needed for inspections and closeout.

The automation concept was simple: if a contractor, supplier, inspection, or cost item could delay the project, it needed to be tracked, assigned, and followed up on systematically.

This allowed me to apply my computer science background to a real-world construction and investment problem.

## Key Execution Challenges

The most important execution challenge was not one single construction item. It was taking control of a project that someone else had started, mismanaged, and abandoned.

That required solving several connected problems:

### Permitting Recovery

The permits had to be transferred, old contractors had to be removed, and new contractors had to be attached properly so work could continue.

### Inspection Recovery

Because the previous builder skipped or mishandled certain inspections, the project required careful review of what had passed, what had failed, and what needed to be reopened or corrected.

### Site Recovery

The property had been left overgrown and poorly maintained, so the construction environment had to be cleaned up and made workable again.

### Flood-Zone Documentation

The project required attention to flood-zone/FEMA-related requirements and city documentation.

### Cost and Scope Control

Because distressed projects can easily bleed money through rework, delays, and contractor ambiguity, I had to manage the budget carefully and protect the investment thesis.

### Contractor Coordination

The project required coordinating multiple trades and replacing or managing prior contractor involvement while keeping progress moving.

## Financial Outcome

The project was acquired for approximately $150,000 and completed all-in for approximately $270,000, including my builder fee. It was later refinanced at approximately $399,000.

The refinance allowed investor capital to be repaid, left approximately $24,000-$25,000 in cash proceeds, and preserved over $100,000 in equity.

The property was also positioned to cash flow. While working toward longer-term rental placement, one unit was listed on Airbnb and averaged approximately $1,700-$1,800 per month, while the second unit was kept open for showings. Market rents were approximately $3,200 per month combined, compared against an estimated monthly payment of approximately $2,700.

## Result

The project converted a distressed, partially abandoned construction site into a completed income-producing duplex.

The investment succeeded because the project was not approached as a simple construction job. It was approached as an operating problem involving research, risk pricing, contractor coordination, permitting, inspections, financing, and systems.

## What This Proves

This project proves I can identify overlooked opportunities, analyze risk, coordinate execution, manage contractors, use systems to control complexity, and create measurable value in real-world assets.

It also reflects my broader career thesis: old-school, asset-heavy industries reward people who understand both the work and the systems. My advantage is being able to combine field execution, software thinking, and operator judgment to improve outcomes.
